Abstract

This project investigates the behavioral and social drivers of free-to-premium conversion on High Note, a freemium music streaming platform. Using a dataset of 43,827 users, we apply Welch's t-tests for descriptive comparison, Propensity Score Matching (PSM) to identify the causal effect of peer influence, and logistic regression across five model specifications to isolate independent predictors of adoption. Our central finding: having at least one premium subscriber friend causes a statistically significant +9.12 percentage point increase in conversion probability after removing 3.39 pp of selection bias. The full model achieves an AUC of 0.812, with subscriber friend count, loved tracks, shouts, and songs listened as the strongest predictors.

Methodology

Analysis proceeds in four stages — from descriptive profiling to causal inference to predictive modeling.

📊
Descriptive Statistics
Welch's two-sample t-tests compared adopters vs. non-adopters across all 14 variables, confirming significant differences at p < 0.001 for every feature.
🪜
Participation Ladder
A behavioral framework organizing user activity from passive listening to active social participation: Songs → Loved Tracks → Playlists → Posts → Shouts.
⚖️
Propensity Score Matching
Nearest-neighbor 1:1 PSM on 13 covariates isolated the causal peer influence effect, matching 9,823 treated users to remove selection bias.
🔢
Logistic Regression
Five model specifications (demographics, consumption, social, network, and full) were compared by AUC and McFadden pseudo R², using VIF to rule out multicollinearity.
02

Key Findings

Causal

PSM reveals that peer influence is causal, not merely correlational. Having at least one premium subscriber friend causes a +9.12 pp increase in conversion probability (95% CI: [8.18%, 10.06%], p < 0.001), after removing 3.39 pp of selection bias from the raw 12.51 pp gap.

Strongest Predictor

Subscriber friend count has the highest odds ratio in the full model at 1.82× — each additional premium friend raises conversion odds by 82%, holding all else constant. Loved tracks (1.61×) and shouts (1.44×) follow closely.

Engagement

Premium adopters listen to 91.9% more songs (33,758 vs. 17,589) and love 204% more tracks than free users. The engagement gap widens as behaviors become more social — shouts differ by +232%, posts by +300%.

Social Network

The Participation Ladder shows that the conversion signal strengthens at each social tier. Passive listeners show the smallest gap between free and premium users; socially interactive behaviors (posts, shouts) show the largest gap.

Counterintuitive

Users from "good country" markets are less likely to convert after controlling for other factors (OR = 0.84×, p = 0.003). Geographic market quality cuts against naive geographic segmentation and warrants caution in targeting strategy.

Strategic Recommendations

An integrated free-to-fee strategy grounded in causal evidence from PSM and logistic regression.

01 / SOCIAL
Target Bridge Users
Prioritize free users with ≥1 subscriber friend for upgrade prompts and free trials. PSM proves a causal +9.12 pp lift from this trait alone.
02 / PRODUCT
Promote Playlist Creation
Playlist creation remains independently significant in the full model. Design features that push users up the Participation Ladder toward content creation.
03 / TARGETING
Activate Heavy Listeners
songsListened and lovedTracks are independent predictors. Time upgrade prompts to listening milestones and tailor messaging by content preference.
04 / EFFICIENCY
Skip Passive Listeners
Passive listeners show the smallest free/premium gap. Concentrate conversion spend on socially active users who are behaviorally closest to willingness to pay.
